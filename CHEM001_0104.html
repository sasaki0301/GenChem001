Classifying Matter

Chapter 1: Matter and Measurement | Estimated Time: 25 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between pure substances and mixtures
  • Differentiate elements and compounds
  • Classify mixtures as homogeneous or heterogeneous

1. Pure Substances vs Mixtures

Matter can be broadly classified into pure substances and mixtures based on composition.

Key Idea: Pure substances have fixed composition, while mixtures have variable composition.

Pure substances vs mixtures differ in composition and uniformity.

  • Pure Substance: same composition throughout (e.g., water, oxygen)
  • Mixture: combination of substances physically combined

Quick Check

Is saltwater a pure substance or a mixture?

Mixture

2. Elements and Compounds

Pure substances can be further classified into elements and compounds.

  • Element: made of one type of atom (e.g., oxygen)
  • Compound: made of two or more elements chemically combined (e.g., water)
Key Idea: Compounds have fixed ratios and can only be separated chemically.

Understanding elements and compounds through particle models.

Concept Check

Why is water considered a compound and not a mixture?

Because hydrogen and oxygen are chemically bonded in a fixed ratio (H₂O).

3. Types of Mixtures

Mixtures can be classified based on uniformity:

  • Homogeneous: uniform throughout (e.g., saltwater)
  • Heterogeneous: non-uniform (e.g., sand and water)
Key Idea: Homogeneous mixtures appear as a single phase, while heterogeneous mixtures have visible components.

Challenge Problem

A mixture looks uniform but contains different substances. Is it homogeneous or heterogeneous?

Homogeneous, because it appears uniform even though it contains multiple substances.

Key Terms

  • Pure Substance: Fixed composition
  • Mixture: Physical combination of substances
  • Element: One type of atom
  • Compound: Chemically combined elements
  • Homogeneous: Uniform mixture
  • Heterogeneous: Non-uniform mixture
