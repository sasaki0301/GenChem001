Explaining the Particle Nature of Matter

Chapter 1: Matter and Measurement | Estimated Time: 20 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Describe matter in terms of particles
  • Explain how particle motion relates to temperature and energy
  • Use the particle model to explain observable phenomena

1. What Is Matter Made Of?

All matter—whether solid, liquid, or gas—is made up of extremely tiny particles. These particles may be atoms, molecules, or ions, depending on the substance.

Key Idea: Matter is not continuous—it is composed of discrete particles separated by space.

Although we cannot see these particles directly, their existence explains many everyday observations such as diffusion, expansion, and changes of state.

Quick Check

Are particles in matter touching each other completely with no space?

No. There is always space between particles, even in solids.

2. Motion of Particles

Particles are in constant motion. The type of motion depends on the state of matter:

  • In solids, particles vibrate in fixed positions.
  • In liquids, particles move past each other.
  • In gases, particles move freely and rapidly in all directions.
Key Idea: Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles.

As temperature increases, particles move faster. This explains why substances expand when heated.

Concept Check

Why does a balloon expand when heated?

Heating increases the kinetic energy of gas particles, causing them to move faster and spread farther apart, increasing volume.

3. Interactions Between Particles

Particles are not only separated by space—they also exert forces on each other. These forces can be attractive or repulsive.

  • Strong attractive forces → particles stay close (solids)
  • Moderate forces → particles can move but remain close (liquids)
  • Weak forces → particles move freely (gases)
Key Idea: The balance between particle motion and attractive forces determines the state of matter.

Challenge Problem

Explain why gases are highly compressible while solids are not.

Gas particles are far apart with large empty spaces, so they can be compressed. Solid particles are tightly packed with little space, making them difficult to compress.

Check Your Understanding

  • How does particle motion differ in solids, liquids, and gases?
  • What does temperature measure at the particle level?
  • Why do particles spread out more in gases?

Key Terms

  • Particle: A small unit of matter such as an atom or molecule
  • Kinetic Energy: Energy due to motion
  • Intermolecular Forces: Forces between particles
